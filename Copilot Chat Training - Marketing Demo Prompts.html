WARM-UP DEMOS

Demo 1: Beverage Industry Trend Briefing (Web Reasoning)
Time:5 min  |  Skill shown:Web grounding, structured output  |  Files needed:None
Open with this — it's fast, impressive, and shows that Copilot Chat (Basic) gives you fresh, cited web data without any uploads. Perfect ice-breaker for marketers who want to know "what's hot right now."
Prompt 1 — Quick trend scan
Act as a senior marketing strategist for a non-alcoholic beverage company. Give me the top 5 consumer trends shaping the beverage industry right now — functional ingredients, premium positioning, sustainability, gen-Z preferences, and anything else that's gaining momentum. For each trend include a one-sentence "why it matters for marketing" implication. Keep it concise and use bullet points.
Prompt 2 — Drill in
Take the top trend you just listed and expand it. What is the size of the opportunity, who are 3 brands leading the trend, what messaging themes are working, and what is one risk a marketer should plan around? Cite your sources.
Prompt 3 — Make it actionable
Now turn this into a 90-second talking-points memo I could read aloud at a Monday marketing stand-up. Lead with the headline, give 3 supporting facts, and end with one decision I want the team to make this week.

Demo 2: Build a Buyer Persona for a New Sparkling Beverage
Time:5 min  |  Skill shown:Iterative prompting, role framing  |  Files needed:None
This shows the value of layered prompting — start broad, refine, then push for an output you can actually use. Marketers love personas; this one builds in 60 seconds.
Prompt 1 — Frame the persona
I'm launching a new line of premium sparkling water with adaptogens (think mood-and-focus benefits) priced 30% above mainstream sparkling water. Build a primary buyer persona for this product. Include: name, age range, occupation, lifestyle, motivations, where they hang out online, the brands they already trust, and 3 emotional triggers a marketer can pull. Make it feel like a real person, not a stat sheet.
Prompt 2 — Add the secondary persona
Now create a contrasting secondary persona who would also buy this product but for very different reasons. Highlight where the messaging needs to flex between the two personas.
Prompt 3 — Stress-test the persona
Pressure-test the primary persona. Where is it weak or potentially stereotypical? What 3 questions should I validate with real customer research before I bet a campaign budget on this profile?
CONTENT CREATION DEMOS

Demo 3: Multi-Channel Campaign for a Craft Soda Launch
Time:6 min  |  Skill shown:Channel-specific tone, copy generation  |  Files needed:None
This is the big "wow" for any marketing audience. One prompt produces a full content set across channels — the time savings story sells itself. Encourage trainees to copy/paste the outputs into a Word doc afterwards.
Prompt 1 — The launch announcement
I'm launching a craft soda called "Glasshouse" — a small-batch botanical soda with hibiscus, ginger, and a touch of cane sugar. Target audience is 25–40 year olds who care about real ingredients and design-forward brands. Write a 90-word launch announcement that I can use as the press-release lead paragraph. Make it confident, sensory, and avoid clichés like "shake up the industry."
Prompt 2 — The content stack
Now adapt the same launch into 4 different formats: (1) an Instagram caption with 5 relevant hashtags, (2) a 2-line LinkedIn post for our founder, (3) a 280-character X post with strong hook, (4) a 4-line headline-and-subhead for a paid Meta ad. Keep brand voice consistent across all four.
Prompt 3 — The retailer pitch email
Draft a 150-word email from our brand manager to a regional grocery chain's beverage buyer, pitching Glasshouse for a 90-day pilot in 25 stores. Lead with what's in it for them (margin, foot traffic, demographic fit), not a feature list. Include a clear next-step ask.
Prompt 4 — The 6-week activation plan
Build a 6-week launch activation plan as a week-by-week table. Columns: Week, Theme, Owner, Channels, Key Asset, Success Metric. Make Week 1 a teaser, Weeks 2–3 the launch peak, Weeks 4–5 momentum, and Week 6 a community/UGC push.

Demo 4: Competitive Brand Teardown
Time:6 min  |  Skill shown:Web grounding, structured competitive analysis  |  Files needed:None — pure web grounding
Pure web-grounded reasoning, no uploads required — perfect for non-licensed Copilot Chat. Show how iterative prompting turns a generic competitor scan into a sharp strategic teardown. Emphasize the citations: marketers can verify every claim before a leadership meeting.
Prompt 1 — Map the competitive field
Map the top 5 premium hard-seltzer brands competing for 25–45 year-old drinkers right now. For each: positioning line, hero flavor, channel mix (DTC, retail, on-premise), and one signature campaign in the last 12 months with what made it work. Cite sources for any specific claim.
Prompt 2 — Find the white space
Where are all 5 starting to sound the same? Identify three positioning gaps that none of them are owning, and for each gap describe the underlying audience tension it would solve. Be specific — "wellness" is not a gap; "low-effort hydration that still feels celebratory" is.
Prompt 3 — Stress-test the angle
Pick the most defensible gap from the previous answer. Give me three reasons it could fail, three early signals we'd watch in market, and one cheap pilot we could run in 30 days to validate or kill the idea before committing real budget.

Demo 5: Press Release & Stakeholder Talking Points
Time:6 min  |  Skill shown:Long-form writing, audience adaptation, tone control  |  Files needed:None
Show how one core story becomes four very different pieces of content for four very different audiences. The point isn't the press release — it's the speed of the audience pivots. Marketers usually do these one at a time over half a day; Copilot does them in 4 minutes.
Prompt 1 — Draft the press release
Write a 300-word press release announcing "Glasshouse" — a small-batch botanical soda made with hibiscus, ginger, and cane sugar, launching summer 2026 in 25 markets. Include a quote from the founder, a sensory product description, and a clear news hook. Tone: confident, journalistic, no marketing-speak.
Prompt 2 — Internal sales rally email
Now rewrite the same story as a 200-word internal email to the field sales team. Energetic and direct. Focus on what's in it for them: margin expectations, demo support available, launch incentives. Bullet the top three "how to sell it" moves they can use this week.
Prompt 3 — Retail buyer one-pager
Convert the same story into a one-page retailer pitch for a national grocery buyer. Lead with category data and consumer trend tailwinds, then shelf positioning, then a 90-day pilot ask in 25 stores. Tone: numbers-first, confident, no fluff. End with a clear yes/no decision the buyer can make.
Prompt 4 — Social caption set
Finally, write five social captions about the launch — 2 Instagram, 2 LinkedIn, 1 TikTok hook. Each one ≤180 characters, no hashtag soup, and each one a distinct angle: heritage, ritual, ingredients, taste, craft. Don't repeat phrases across captions.
FILE-GROUNDED DEMOS

Demo 6: Analyze Uploaded Marketing Performance Data
Time:6 min  |  Skill shown:File grounding (uploaded files), reasoning over data  |  Files needed:Beverage_Q1_Campaign_Performance.xlsx (sample provided in trainer's pack)
Important framing: with Copilot Chat (Basic), uploaded files ARE supported — that's the work-data substitute for non-licensed users. Tell them: drag a file into the chat, and Copilot reasons over it. Don't mention "Analyst agent" — that's a licensed-only feature.
Prompt 1 — Read the file
I've uploaded a Q1 marketing performance file for a beverage brand. Read every tab. Give me a 6-bullet executive summary: total spend, total revenue attributed, blended ROAS, top-performing channel, weakest-performing channel, and the single biggest surprise in the data.
Prompt 2 — Find the story
Now go deeper. Compare campaigns by channel and audience segment. Where are we overspending relative to revenue returned? Where do we have hidden upside — campaigns with weak spend but strong conversion? Give me 3 specific reallocation moves I should make next quarter, each with the rationale grounded in numbers from the file.
Prompt 3 — Build the executive narrative
Turn your analysis into a 200-word executive narrative I can paste into a quarterly review email to my CMO. Lead with the headline result, support it with 3 numbers, and end with the recommended decision and what we need approval on.

Demo 7: Customer Survey & Voice-of-Customer Insights
Time:5 min  |  Skill shown:Unstructured text reasoning, theme extraction  |  Files needed:Beverage_Customer_Survey_Responses.docx (sample provided)
This one always lands. Marketers spend hours reading verbatims. This demo shows Copilot doing it in 30 seconds — and pulling out signals a human reader would miss.
Prompt 1 — Find the themes
I've uploaded open-ended survey responses from beverage customers. Identify the top 5 recurring themes. For each theme, give me: a name, a one-sentence summary, the approximate share of responses that mention it, and one direct quote that captures the sentiment best.
Prompt 2 — Find what's surprising
What did customers say that we were NOT expecting? Look for unusual mentions — packaging complaints, surprising use occasions, unexpected emotional language, or themes that contradict our usual assumptions about beverage buyers. Be specific and quote them.
Prompt 3 — Convert to creative inspiration
Now turn those themes into 3 creative campaign concepts a creative team could brief out. For each concept, give it a name, a one-line idea, the core insight from the survey it's built on, and the headline tagline.
OUTLOOK & MEMORY DEMOS

Demo 8: Copilot Chat in Outlook — Manage the Marketing Inbox
Time:5 min  |  Skill shown:Outlook reasoning over inbox & calendar (non-licensed)  |  Where to run:Outlook web, new Outlook Windows, Mac, or mobile
This is THE killer demo for non-licensed users. As of early 2026, Copilot Chat in Outlook reasons over the entire inbox, calendar, and meetings — even without a paid license. And per Microsoft's May 16, 2026 changes, Outlook is the ONE app where Copilot Chat stays even for enterprise non-licensed users. Lean into this.
Prompt 1 — Catch up on the morning
Summarize my unread emails from the last 24 hours by priority. Group them into: (1) needs a reply today, (2) FYI / no action needed, (3) can be archived. For each item in group 1, give me a one-sentence reply suggestion I could send.
Prompt 2 — Pull marketing-specific signals
Across my inbox, find anything related to our upcoming product launch. Pull out: open questions waiting on me, decisions that have been made, deadlines mentioned, and anyone who looks blocked. Format as a one-page status digest.
Prompt 3 — Calendar prep
Look at my calendar for tomorrow. For each meeting, tell me: who's in it, what it's about based on related emails, what I should prepare, and one smart question I could ask to add value.
Prompt 4 — Draft a follow-up
Find the most recent thread about our packaging redesign. Draft a follow-up email summarizing what was decided, listing open items, and proposing a 30-minute working session next week to close the remaining questions. Match the tone of the previous emails in the thread.

Demo 9: Copilot Memory — Personalize Your Marketing Assistant
Time:4 min  |  Skill shown:Saved memories, custom instructions  |  Where:Copilot Chat → Settings → Personalization
Memory is available to ALL Copilot Chat users — licensed and non-licensed alike. Walk through the Settings → Personalization page first, then run these prompts. Memories are stored in the user's own Exchange mailbox in a hidden folder, so they follow standard enterprise data protection.
Prompt 1 — Teach Copilot about your role
Please remember the following about my role so future answers fit my context: I work in marketing for a beverage company. My audience is mostly 25–45 year-old consumers. I write for an internal CMO audience and external trade press. I prefer outputs in concise bullets, with a one-line "headline" before any list, and I always want a clear "what to decide" at the end.
Prompt 2 — Teach it your brand voice
Remember our brand voice rules: confident but never cocky, sensory and specific over abstract, no buzzwords like "synergy" or "leverage," prefer active verbs, and always avoid clichés like "game-changer" or "shake up the industry."
Prompt 3 — Test the memory
Now, draft a 60-word internal note announcing that we're launching a new low-sugar variant of our top-selling juice. Use what you know about my role and our brand voice.
Prompt 4 — Manage the memories
What do you currently remember about me? List everything in your memory so I can review and clean it up.
STRETCH / BONUS DEMO

Demo 10 (Bonus): Image Concept Generation for a Beverage Campaign
Time:3 min  |  Skill shown:Image generation in Copilot Chat  |  Note:Image generation is included in Copilot Chat (Basic)
Use this if you have time at the end. Marketers love seeing visual output. Remind them generated images are for ideation only — final creative needs the agency or in-house design team.
Prompt 1 — Set the visual mood
Generate three early concept visuals for a summer campaign for a premium sparkling water brand. Concept A: minimalist, editorial, lots of negative space. Concept B: vibrant, playful, mid-century pop art. Concept C: cinematic, golden-hour, lifestyle. No people in any of the images — just product, environment, and mood.
Prompt 2 — Iterate on the winner
Take Concept B and produce three variations: one for a Times Square billboard, one for an Instagram square post, and one for a vertical mobile story. Keep the visual identity consistent.
