Part 1 · 15 min

What Makes a Great Copilot Prompt

~15 min
Concept Why Prompting Matters ▼

Copilot is only as good as what you ask.

  • Vague prompts → generic, unhelpful responses
  • Specific prompts → save hours every week
Quality Description
MEH Vague one-liner
OK Some context added
WOW Goal + Context + Format + Source
Framework The 4 Elements of a Great Prompt ▼
  1. 1 Goal — What do you want Copilot to do?
  2. 2 Context — Who’s the audience? What’s the background?
  3. 3 Expectations — Format, length, tone, constraints
  4. 4 Source — Which data, files, or emails to use
Examples Prompt Makeover: Before & After ▼

Email

Before (Vague)
Write an email about the project
After (Great)
Draft a concise 3-paragraph email to my project stakeholders summarizing this week's progress on the Azure migration, noting the 2-day delay in Phase 2, and requesting feedback by Friday. Tone: professional but direct.

Meeting

Before (Vague)
Prep me for my meeting
After (Great)
I have a 30-minute 1:1 with my manager Cristi Bennett tomorrow. Review my last 3 emails to her, any action items she's assigned me, and my calendar for this week. Give me a 5-bullet brief.

Document

Before (Vague)
Write a summary
After (Great)
Summarize the key decisions and open questions from /Finance Q1 Review.docx in bullet format, no longer than one page, for an exec audience.

Demo 1 Triage Your Inbox — Outlook ▼

Scenario: Monday morning, 47 unread emails

Live Prompt
Summarize my unread emails from the last 48 hours. Highlight anything urgent, flag action items, and tell me what I can ignore.

Expected Outcome

  • Urgent items surfaced immediately — no inbox scrolling
  • Action items extracted and listed by priority
  • Low-priority threads identified for bulk archive
  • Time saved: 20–30 minutes of manual triage

Scenario: QBR with your VP in 30 minutes

Live Prompt
I have a QBR with my VP in 30 minutes. Check my last 5 emails related to our Q1 results. Give me a 5-point brief with any risks I should flag.

Expected Outcome

  • 5-bullet executive brief ready in under 60 seconds
  • Risks and open items surfaced from your emails
  • You walk in prepared — not scrambling for context

Expected Outcome

  • Full draft generated in seconds — grounded in your files
  • Proper structure: status, blockers, next steps
  • VP-ready tone applied automatically

Best Practices & The Copilot Playbook

~10 min
Best Practices Top 8 Copilot Best Practices ▼
  1. 1Always give context — who, what, why
  2. 2Specify your audience and format
  3. 3Iterate — refine prompts when first answer isn’t right
  4. 4Reference specific files with /
  5. 5Review everything before sending
  6. 6Use Copilot to catch your own mistakes
  7. 7Start every morning with a briefing
  8. 8Share great prompts with your team
Pitfalls Common Pitfalls to Avoid ▼
Don’t Do Instead
Send without reviewing Always review AI-generated content
Use vague one-liners Add context, format, and audience
Repeat the same failing prompt Change one variable and retry
Rely on Copilot for facts Verify statistics and quotes
Ignore the /file reference Ground Copilot in YOUR data
Playbook The Copilot Playbook ▼
  1. 1START — Replace 1 manual task per day with Copilot
  2. 2BUILD — Create a personal prompt library
  3. 3SCALE — Share wins, build team playbooks

Memory & Custom Instructions

Setup Custom Instructions in Settings ▼

Access Custom Instructions in Settings → Personalization. Copilot uses these to tailor every response to your preferences.

Below are example custom instructions organized by category. Copy each one into your Custom Instructions to personalize Copilot.

Instructions Core Principles ▼
Custom Instruction
Core Principles Copilot prioritizes accuracy, clarity, and trust. Always distinguish verified facts from inferred reasoning. Never present assumptions or context-based reasoning as fact. Avoid cascade errors by rechecking each step before responding. Think deeply before answering. When uncertain, ask clarifying questions or present two to three vetted options with pros and cons.
Instructions Tone by Audience ▼
Custom Instruction
Tone by Audience Internal or Collaborative Audience Collaborative and supportive. Slightly informal and pragmatic. Quick confirmations and concise language. Use only when the audience is clearly internal. Example language: "Thanks for sharing." "Would it work to proceed this way?" "Appreciate your support." Customer or External Audience Professional, courteous, and reassuring. Formal greetings and closings. Avoid jargon unless contextually relevant and explained. No slang or overly casual language. Provide clear next steps and timelines. Example language: "Looking forward to discussing the way forward." "We completely understand the need to address this carefully." "Sounds like a great opportunity."
Instructions Content Labels ▼
Custom Instruction
Content Labels Use explicit labels consistently. [Verified] Facts confirmed by authoritative sources. [Inference] Logical conclusions based on context. [Confidence] Confidence cues for inferred content such as "Likely" or "Based on observed patterns." [Unverified] Claims not yet confirmed. [Glossary] Acronyms clarified on first use. When mixing content, present verified facts first, then clearly disclose inferred content with this disclaimer: "The following points are inferred based on available context and may require validation."
Instructions Data Sourcing & Accuracy ▼
Custom Instruction
Data Sourcing and Accuracy Search enterprise content first. Files. Emails. Chats. Meetings. Transcripts. Resolve contradictions before responding. Preserve critical qualifiers in summaries. Never fabricate citations, quotes, or references. If confidence is low, state what is known, state what is unknown, recommend next steps. For legal or regulatory topics: verify jurisdiction and verify effective dates. For time sensitive topics: check date stamps, warn about staleness, add freshness disclaimers when appropriate. If no authoritative source exists: state "No authoritative source found" and propose next steps such as further internal or external research. Never present inferences as facts.
Instructions Output Guidelines ▼
Custom Instruction
Output Guidelines Always include a TLDR at the top of emails and documents. Present information in a logical order: verified facts first, inferences second, recommendations and next steps last. Use clear section headings. Avoid decorative characters such as hyphens when writing text. Be concise while preserving precision. Never guess or fill gaps with plausible language. State clearly when information is missing. Surface multiple interpretations when they exist and explain tradeoffs. Favor clarity and honesty over completeness when certainty is low.

Power Tips

Additional Useful Prompts

Prompt Plain Text Output ▼

When you want the output to be just a block of text (no formatting):

Prompt
Output the results only as a block of plain text. Do not include any other text. Always treat the block of plain text as a block of code that uses text as the code language. Always include a copy button to copy the contents of the box. Do not include sources.
Prompt Get the Resultant Prompt ▼

Useful when running out of iterations (maximum of 30 iterations per chat). Get the prompt that got you to the current result so you can continue in a new chat:

Prompt
What is the resultant prompt so far?
Prompt Blocking Off Calendar ▼

Quickly block time on your calendar without switching apps:

Prompt
Place the following hold on my calendar. Call it 'HOLD: Important Meeting' No agenda, no attendees. Just trying to block time that I offered for a QBR. All day event. The date is 6/15/2026.

New & Coming Soon

Available Now Edit with Copilot ▼

Inline AI editing directly within your documents. Select text, choose an action, Copilot rewrites in place.

  • Rewrite for tone, length, or clarity

  • Fix grammar and polish language
  • Accept, reject, or refine suggestions

Example

Before “The thing about this project is that it has been going on for a while and there have been a lot of issues.”
Copilot action Make this concise and professional
After “This project has experienced ongoing delays and significant challenges.”

Available now in Word, Outlook, Teams, and PowerPoint
